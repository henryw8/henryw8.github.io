About

I’m passionate about using data and technology to make sports more transparent while gaining a competitive edge. Growing up as a mathlete, competitive swimmer, and an avid Philadelphia sports fan, I’ve always appreciated the harmony between the game and the numbers we use to describe it.

Today, I split my time between MIT’s Sports Lab, where I’m working with FIFA to build systems that assist referees on the pitch, and the Boston Red Sox, where I focus on extracting hitting and pitcing insights from our motion-capture data.

MIT Sports Lab

September 2023 - Present

At the MIT Sports Lab, I'm working on making advanced sports technology more accessible and fair. My primary project involves developing automated systems to help referees make better decisions in soccer, using optical tracking data to detect last touches and classify fouls.

I also research how analytics adoption impacts team performance in professional sports. One recent project used causal inference methods to show that NBA teams significantly undervalue their analytics departments compared to players—a finding published in the Journal of Sports Economics.

As the lab's liaison to FIFA, I present our research at international conferences and help bridge the gap between academic innovation and practical implementation for FIFA's 211 member associations.

Featured Work

Bringing AI to Soccer Officiating

MIT Sports Lab × FIFA

Leading the development of automated referee assistance systems using optical tracking. The goal? Help referees make better decisions on last-touch calls and fouls, starting with technology that works for leagues at all levels, not just those with multi-million dollar budgets.

I've presented this work at the FIFA Research Symposium and MIT Sports Summit, and it's incredibly rewarding to see how it could impact the game at every level.

The Value of Analytics in the NBA

Published in Journal of Sports Economics, 2025

Using causal inference methods, I found that NBA teams significantly undervalue their analytics departments compared to players. The research shows that adding an analyst has a comparable impact on wins as many mid-tier players, but at a fraction of the cost.

It's a reminder that the smartest teams aren't always the ones with the biggest budgets—they're the ones who know where to invest.

Decoding Pitcher Deception

Boston Red Sox Baseball Sciences

Working with biomechanical tracking data to understand what makes some pitchers harder to hit. I've developed deep learning models that quantify deception and built tools that help coaches quickly compare mechanics across pitchers.

The best part? Seeing coaches use these tools to help players develop in real-time.
